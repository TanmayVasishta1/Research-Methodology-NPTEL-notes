📘 WEEK 5

Creativity in Research & Ethics in Research

🎨 Creativity in Research — Part 1

What is a Fully Professional Researcher?

Has something to say that international peers want to listen to
Has command over their area/subject
Has fundamental concepts that international peers are interested in
Peer group must be truly international — not just local
Should be among the top 10-20 experts in their specific domain
Should be aware of situations where they can make significant contribution
Must have mastery of appropriate techniques (analytical, numerical, experimental)
Must be able to communicate ideas effectively through papers & presentations
Should be a fountain head of ideas — generating new ideas throughout lifetime
Should be able to guide Ph.D. students and defend proposals
A fully professional researcher does NOT work without focusing on literature. They have deep knowledge AND broad awareness of their field globally.

Sternberg's Triarchic Theory of Intelligence (1985, Yale University)

From: Beyond IQ: A Triarchic Theory of Human Intelligence, Cambridge University Press

TypeWhat It IsWhere Tested
Analytical IntelligenceAbility to solve well-defined problems with a single correct answer. Tested through exams, grades.Classrooms, traditional exams
Synthetic IntelligenceAbility to combine existing knowledge in new ways. Off-beat solutions, different people can get different answers.Project work, design problems, research
Practical IntelligenceAbility to adapt to everyday needs, "street smarts". Selling ideas to funding agencies, managers, editors, reviewers. Relates to how one relates to the environment around them.Never formally taught — acquired over years
In traditional education, only Analytical Intelligence is excessively focused. The other two (Synthetic & Practical) must be self-learned. Research success depends heavily on all three.

Role of Knowledge

Knowledge gives us the ability to recognize what is genuinely new
Without knowledge, you may "reinvent the wheel"
You must know what has already been done before you can do something new
Knowledge provides skills to design experiments, analyze results, do scientific computing
Creativity must be supplemented by knowledge — brilliance alone is not enough

Attributes of a Good Researcher

Sound grasp of fundamentals — very important foundation
Mastery of techniques in the field
Tenacity and perseverance — resilience in face of failures and rejections
Scholarship — deep knowledge + awareness of global research landscape
Communication skills — papers, posters, presentations
Creativity — often neglected but essential

What is Creativity?

A creative person does things that have never been done before
Can be in any field — science, engineering, music, law, philosophy
Creativity includes: discoveries of new knowledge, invention of new technology, composing music, novel legal arguments
A creative person will likely find a new approach to solve a problem that others may not have considered
Usually does not conform to pre-existing notions of what is possible
Does NOT mean just accurately repeating work done by others

Ph.D. and Depth vs Width

B.Tech: Know a little about everything
M.Tech: Know reasonably about many things
Ph.D.: Know a LOT about a very narrow area
We gain width through the depth — deep focus cultivates a "vision" (darshan) that improves ability to understand other areas

🎨 Creativity in Research — Part 2

Thinking Styles of Creative People

Creative people often question conventional wisdom, assumptions, and rules
May get into conflicts with society because of non-conformity
Need to be persistent, tenacious, uncompromising, stubborn
Must believe it will work and keep working at it
Self-confidence (arrogance in "homeopathic dose" only!)

The 10,000-Hour Rule — Herbert Simon (Nobel Laureate)

It takes approximately 10,000 hours of deliberate practice/study to master a skill or area of research. This debunks the "Genius Theory" — it's mostly hard work (perspiration).
ScenarioHours/DayTime to Mastery
Practice (e.g., tennis, music)3 hours/day~10 years
Full-time employment8 hours/day~5 years
Research student (intensive)10-12 hours/day, 6 days/week~3-4 years
If you have above average intelligence + put in 10,000 hours → anyone can become expert
Mozart, A.R. Rahman, Viswanathan Anand, Bill Gates, Beatles, Chess Grand Masters — all put in 10,000 hours before becoming famous
Also studied by Prof. Anders Ericsson of Florida State University
Malcolm Gladwell's book "Outliers" (2008) — conclusively established the 10,000-hour rule

Hard Work — The Key

Science has progressed through hard work, failures, and frustrations
Programs don't work first time; experiments fail; papers get rejected
Journal publications do NOT reveal the full story — pain, frustrations, failures are not reported
Research progress follows a log sigmoidal curve — slow initially, then rapid, then plateaus
Society attaches too much importance only to success

Motivation

Extrinsic Motivation
Rewards, money, praise, promotion, prizes, fame
External motivators that propel work
Good starting point for beginners
Intrinsic Motivation
Self-set goals, achieve, set new goals
The sheer joy of solving an unknown riddle
"Eureka feeling" — not related to money/degree
More important in highly creative people

Role of Stress in Research — Yerkes-Dodson Curve (1908)

We work to the best of our potential when we are Eustressed (optimally/reasonably stressed). Optimal stress propels us to work harder and perform at peak levels.
Stress LevelNamePerformance
Too LowHypo-stressedVery Low — no motivation
OptimalEustressed (eu + stress = good stress)🔥 Peak Performance
Too HighHyper-stressed / DistressedVery Low — anxiety, breakdown
Achievement = optimal nervousness + channelizing disenchantment
Some optimal unhappiness with current state propels improvement
Stress is NOT stressful until it becomes distress

How Does Creativity Occur?

Often occurs in an intuitive flash of insight — "Dharshana" (vision)
Can come when NOT in the lab — jogging, watching movie, taking bath
Max Planck example: Got dharshana of "minus 1" in black body radiation → led to quantum mechanics → Nobel Prize (1918)
Write down ideas immediately when a brain wave comes
Have backup plans — if an idea doesn't work in 2-3 weeks, move on

Medawar Zone — Sir Peter Medawar (Nobel Prize 1960, Medicine)

Y-axis: Payoff; X-axis: Difficulty level
Very easy problems → low return
Very difficult problems → low return (may not solve)
Right difficulty → highest return = the Medawar Zone
The right research problem should be neither trivially easy nor impossibly hard

Flow Theory — Prof. Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi (Univ. of Chicago)

Y-axis: Challenge; X-axis: Skill
High skill + Low challenge → Boredom
Low skill + High challenge → Anxiety
Challenge = Skill → FLOW (peak performance, timelessness, enjoyment)
According to flow theory, a person performs best when challenge is equal to the skill
Navigate life so challenge and skill stay matched → you'll be happy and productive

Experienced researchers (20+ years) tend to be less creative due to familiarity

Ignorance of basic education does NOT enhance creativity — knowledge is essential
Nobel Prize not given to upstarts — results must withstand test of time (20-30 years)

⚖️ Ethics in Research

What is Ethics?

Derived from Greek word "ethos" meaning character
Good character = moral integrity of the researcher
Important for ensuring reliable process with trustworthy and valid findings
Research depends on public trust

Ethical Aspects Govern:

How research is funded
How research is carried out
How research is reported
How and what information is shared

The Research Process & Ethics

Problem ID → Literature Review → Objectives → Methodology → Analysis → Publishing
Ethics plays a role at every step from problem identification to publishing

Key Ethical Issues

IssueDescription
PlagiarismTaking someone else's work and presenting it as your own without proper acknowledgement
Fabrication of DataMaking up data or results that don't exist (e.g., Hwang Woo-suk case)
Breach of ConfidentialityDisclosing personal information of participants without consent
DiscriminationBased on gender, caste, race, nationality, language (e.g., Tuskegee study)
Authorship IssuesNot all authors knowing/agreeing to publication; gift authorship

Unethical Behavior in Research Includes:

Copying from other papers (plagiarism)
Directly changing data if it is not as expected (fabrication/falsification)
Accusing other researchers to become prominent (making unfounded charges)
NOT: "Listing all the authors while writing a paper" — that is ethical!

Reasons for Unethical Practices

Pressure to become famous
Intention to have quick career growth
Pressure to get funding
Self-interest, ignorance, relativism
Pressure to publish ("publish or perish")
Poor supervision of students
NOT: "The need to teach well" — that is not a reason for unethical practices

Case Study: Prof. Hwang Woo-suk (South Korea)

2004: Claimed to have cloned human embryo & extracted stem cells (in journal Science)
Called South Korea's "national treasure"
Investigation found: faked claims, fabricated data, coerced female lab members
2006: Fired from Seoul National University, charged with fraud & embezzlement

Historical Milestones in Research Ethics

EventYearKey Contribution
Nuremberg Code1949Emphasis on voluntary consent from participants
Tuskegee Syphilis Study1932-1972Exposed race-based discrimination; no consent/treatment given to Black subjects
Declaration of Helsinki1964Mandated risk-benefit analysis, respect for privacy, minimize cost to subjects
Belmont Report1979Voluntary consent, autonomy of individual, protection of human subjects
Animal Welfare Act—Guidelines for research with animals; respect animal life

Publishing Ethics

Important to ensure experiments were conducted correctly
Important to ensure references are properly cited
Only people involved in the research should be acknowledged as authors
UNETHICAL: Agreement with colleague to include names in each other's papers without involvement
If collaborators generate data together, it is NOT ethical for some to publish independently without all authors knowing and agreeing. All authors should know and agree with the publication.

Researcher's Obligations

Honesty — to self, work, society, fellow researchers
Integrity — moral soundness
Minimal risk to participants and themselves
Cultural sensitivity
Transparency
Responsibility — the most important concept in research ethics
Social benefits > individual benefits

📗 WEEK 6

Intellectual Property Rights (IPR)

🏛️ Intellectual Property Rights — Part 1

IP vs Real Property

Real Property
Tangible — can touch & feel
Has physical boundaries
Exists in time & space
Examples: Land, laptop, pen, house, car
Limits on enjoyment (room capacity)
Rivalrous
Intellectual Property
Intangible — cannot touch & feel
Boundaries defined by legal documents (claims, specifications)
Does NOT exist in time & space like real property
Examples: Inventions, books, trademarks
No limits on enjoyment (millions can read same book)
Non-rivalrous
The idea that distinguishes IP from real property is: Real properties have physical boundaries. They are NOT the same. IP is NOT superior to real property — they are different types.

What is Intellectual Property?

Original ideas, results from research, critical business information
Property that emanates from human creative labour
IP protects applications/expressions of ideas — NOT ideas per se
An original set of ideas or research results falls in the category of IP
Properties emanating from human creativity/labour = IP

What are Rights?

A legal entitlement — something you can get legally
Can be justified, recognized, and protected
Violation is unlawful and leaves the violated party with a remedy
Two senses: Liberty (freedom to do) & License (consent given)
Inherent rights (human rights) vs. rights created by law (property rights)
General rights (shared by all) vs. Exclusive rights (stop others from acting)

Infringement

Violation of an intellectual property right
Technically means trespass — getting into the property of someone else
Both civil and criminal remedies available
Leads to infringement → can get injunction or monetary damages

Patent Specification & Claims

Patent specification = descriptive document for an invention (like a land deed)
Contains: background art, description, embodiments, illustrations
Claims = concluding part that demarcates boundaries of the IP
Protection is confined to what is mentioned in the claims, not the descriptive part
Every invention needs to be described in writing
Prior art = what has gone before (existing technology)
Inventor must distinguish invention from prior art

"Intellectual" Component

Refers to things from our intellect — mental effort, creative effort
Combination of physical and mental skills — but IP focuses on the mental element
Distinguished as something from human beings, not machines
Example: Carpenter making chair — physical labor visible, but mental conception is the IP

Key Characteristics/Traits of IPR

#TraitExplanation
1Protectable by law (Enforceable)If violated, there is a remedy (injunction or damages)
2Intangible in natureCannot touch/feel; must be described and registered
3Easily replicableFirst copy expensive, subsequent copies nearly free (Windows 7 example)
4Requires creative effortCreations of the mind; often group efforts in modern research
5Has commercial valueCan be used in trade and business
6Non-rivalrousUse by one does not take away enjoyment by another
7Non-excludableOnce public, hard to stop others from accessing it
Common characteristics of IPR: Protectable by law, Intangible in nature, Has commercial value. Note: "Cannot be easily replicated" is INCORRECT — IP CAN be easily replicated (that's the problem IPR solves!).

Definition of IPR

There is no universal/agreed definition of IPR
WIPO (World Intellectual Property Organization) — UN body for IP
WIPO's definition is an "inclusive/grocery list" — lists patents, copyrights, trademarks, designs, etc.
Generally: "Rights that protect applications of ideas and information of commercial value"

Types of IPR

TypeProtectsKey Detail
PatentInventions (technological)Rights: make, sell, use, offer for sale, import
CopyrightLiterary, artistic, musical, cinematographic works, computer programsRights: print, publish, perform, film, record
TrademarkWords, symbols identifying a company/productsHelps identify producer; ambassador of business
DesignShape, configuration, pattern — aesthetic/visual appealNo functional element; test = "what the eye can see"
Geographical IndicationOrigin of goods from specific geographyE.g., Darjeeling tea; beneficiaries = producers from that region
Trade SecretConfidential business informationAs long as kept secret, enforceable

Two Types of Property

Public property — held in common
Private property — held individually

Registration & Its Importance

Registration = the process through which IPR are recognized
We get to know what the right holder has claimed
It is the form by which IP is protected
Done by the government/state — confers sanctity
Copyright exception: Under the Berne Convention, copyright registration is NOT mandatory for enforcement. Just a copyright notice (©, name, year) is sufficient.
Patent specification undergoes patent prosecution in the patent office

🏛️ Intellectual Property Rights — Part 2

Duration of IP Rights

IPR TypeDurationCategory
Patent20 years from date of application (globally, per TRIPS)Limited Life IP
Copyright (India)Life of author + 60 years; Institution: date of creation + 60 yearsLimited Life IP
TrademarkGranted for 10 years; renewable (can be kept alive forever)Unlimited Life IP
Trade SecretAs long as kept confidential — unlimitedUnlimited Life IP
DesignLimited (weakest IPR)Limited Life IP
Key exam points:
• Patent = 20 years from date of application ✓ (TRIPS Agreement)
• Trademark granted for 10 years, renewable — but a trademark has unlimited life (can be renewed indefinitely)
• Copyright India = Life + 60 years
• A trademark is NOT granted for 15 years — it's 10 years!
• IPR did NOT first come up during Green Revolution — they came with Industrial Revolution
• Limited life IP can go to public domain after expiry ✓
• IP is NOT the same as real property ✓
• IPR are exclusive rights (not non-exclusive)

TRIPS Agreement & WTO

TRIPS = Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights
An agreement under the World Trade Organization (WTO)
Mandates 20-year patent term for all WTO member countries
Technology agnostic — applies to ALL fields of technology
Before TRIPS: Patent term was 14 years in most countries
In India before TRIPS: 14 years general, up to 7 years for pharmaceuticals

Why 14 Years Historically?

In England: 7 years to train an apprentice
Craftsmen were given exclusive privilege in exchange for training 2 British apprentices
7 + 7 = 14 years

Historical Context: Industrial Revolution & IPR

IPR attained international recognition with advent of Industrial Revolution
Advancement in printing technology → quick spread of ideas
People crossing borders → translation of works → spread of ideas
Mass manufacturing made duplication easy → need for protection
Before Industrial Revolution: Oral-formulaic tradition (memorize and pass on)

Oral-Formulaic Tradition & Copyright

Knowledge was transmitted by memorization and oral recitation
Shakespeare benefited from this tradition
Copyright regime requires things to be recorded/expressed on some medium
Medium: paper (print), tapes (film), digital
Memorizing something is NOT "making a copy" — copyright requires a medium

Patent Rights — Exclusive Bundle

Right to make/manufacture
Right to sell
Right to use
Right to offer for sale
Right to import
Anyone doing these without consent → infringement

Trademark — Key Points

Identifies a company and its products/services
Can be registered OR established by use (law of passing off)
Even without registration, long-time use gives protection
Well-known marks can stop use even in different business domains (Benz logo for undergarments case)
Mark acts as ambassador for the business
Trademarks = unlimited life IP (keep renewing)

Design Rights

Protects aesthetic appeal — what the eye can see
Does NOT protect functional elements (that's patents' domain)
Used for mass-produced goods (tiles, bottles, jewelry, clothing)
Weakest of IPRs — restrictions on enforcement, limited damages
Example: Children's shoe shaped like bunny = design; Nike shoe with grip features = patent territory

Copyright vs Design

If something can be protected by copyright, nobody goes for shorter-term design
Copyright = life + 60 years (much longer protection)
Design rights exist for industrial designs that can't claim copyright protection

Medical Methods

Medical methods of treatment are exempted from patent protection under Indian Act (Section 3)

Book Written by Prof Feroz

A book can be protected by Copyright
Not by trademark, patent, or design

Which IPR Identifies a Company?

Trademark — it identifies the company and its products

Example of Real Property

House is real property (tangible, physical boundaries)
Invention = IP; Trademark = IP; Car = debatable but exam answer is House
