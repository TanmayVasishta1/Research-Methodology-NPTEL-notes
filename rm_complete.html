Week 1

Introduction to the World of Research & Experimental Skills

🔬 What is Research?

Research is a transition from being a consumer of knowledge to a producer of knowledge. It involves making a novel contribution to a field through self-driven discovery, experimentation, and validation.

Einstein: "If we knew what we were doing, it would not be called research, would it?"
📘 Course-Based Degree

Structured program. Established knowledge through courses & exams. Well-defined path. Everyone aims for correct answers.

🔭 Research Degree

Novel contribution. Self-driven discovery. Open-ended. Unique thesis — cannot be similar to anyone else's. Training to guide future researchers.

Incremental vs Path-Breaking Research

Most research is incremental — small structured additions to knowledge. Path-breaking discoveries happen by serendipity through this steady incremental process. Incremental research leads to path-breaking research — they are connected, not independent.

Exam Point: What distinguishes a research degree = "Specialized knowledge in a specific field" (NOT general knowledge, NOT presentation skills, NOT coursework completion).

🗺️ Selecting a Research Area

Research is like exploring an unknown city. You don't know which monuments will fascinate you until you start exploring.

  1. Start with genuine interest — Passion is your engine. Intrinsic motivation is non-negotiable. Don't let job market or trends drive your choice.
  2. Trace your interest — From a course that resonated, general reading that sparked curiosity, or desire for societal impact.
  3. Specialize beyond the textbook — True research is highly specialized. If a textbook exists for your exact topic, you're probably too late.
  4. Conduct a literature survey — Important early step involving trial and error to identify interesting and novel topics.
Exam Alert: Important factors for selecting area = "The appeal and interest in the area AND the impact it has on the scientific community." NOT convenience of location, schedule flexibility, or number of students.

👨‍🏫 Selecting an Advisor

A PhD can last 4–7 years — this is one of the longest professional partnerships you'll have.

  • Investigate: Read potential advisor's publications. Does their work excite you? What is their publication record?
  • Communicate: Have honest conversations. Express your interests, ask about mentorship style.
  • Evaluate: Working style (hands-on, theoretical, experimental), personality, energy.
Role of Advisor: "To provide guidance when the student encounters difficulties or loses direction." NOT day-to-day instructions, NOT doing all research, NOT ensuring strict adherence to initial plan.

A good advisor helps understand the "lay of the land" — is an area too crowded? Is a problem worth solving?

📓 Day-to-Day Research Practice

  • Most daily work = small steps. Setting up experiments may take 2–3 weeks — that's normal, not wasted time.
  • Reproducing existing work is a valid starting point — it makes you familiar with the area.
  • Data should be believable, defensible, and reproducible.
  • Research Notebook: Record EVERY observation, small result, every plot — electronically or on paper.
  • Be excited and proud of small results — keeps you thinking about extensions.
Misconception busted: Working late nights ≠ great work. Regular 8am–5pm with evenings off can produce excellent research. What matters is regular, disciplined hours.
Exam Point — Attitude: "A steady and incremental approach while keeping the long-term goal in mind." NOT urgency/impatience, NOT OCD, NOT deviating from objectives.

Simulations = Experiments

  • Same rules of Design of Experiments apply to simulations
  • Simulators are "deadly weapons" — can produce massive amounts of data, only a fraction useful
  • Must understand solver settings, engine underneath, right tools for purpose

👥 Peer Review & Validation

Research is NOT Democratic

  • One expert can prove everyone before wrong — their idea then prevails.
  • Not about majority agreement, not about authority of famous names.
  • Peers review each other and hold something as true until disproved.

Validation Hierarchy

  1. Self-validation: Conviction must begin from YOU. Validate qualitatively and quantitatively.
  2. Advisor discussion: Share and discuss — maybe you're right, maybe you need correction.
  3. Group presentation: Share with group mates for critical feedback.
  4. Peer community: Communicate through conferences and papers — identify reputable journals and conferences run by professional societies (e.g., IEEE).
Thomas Huxley: "Science is simply common sense at its best, that is, rigidly accurate in observation, and merciless to fallacy in logic."

Results should be reproducible — publish code, post data if not confidential. You must be the most critical examiner of your own work. There is no "final result" — research fields don't end.

⚗️ Experimental Skills — The Golden Rules

Experiments are supreme — they validate theories. But they must be run correctly. If theory doesn't match well-run experiments, the theory is incomplete.

Temperature Measurement

  • Common error: Trusting furnace display blindly. Thermocouple may be far from your sample.
  • Furnace shows 800°C, but sample could be 750°C due to gas flow, heating element positions.
  • Solution: Add a second thermocouple directly above your sample. Adjust furnace set-point.
  • Temperature gradients exist both vertically and horizontally in furnaces.

Humidity Measurement

  • Common error: Assuming humidifier output = 100% RH. Design and flow rate affect efficiency.
  • Solution: Use a desiccant box — weigh before and after to measure actual water content.
  • If tube drops to room temperature, water condenses out — must heat-trace the path above dew point.

Current & Voltage Measurement

❌ Two-Probe Measurement

Same two contact points for current AND voltage. Measures R_contact1 + R_sample + R_contact2. Can be off by 20–30%!

✅ Four-Probe Measurement

Current through outer two probes, voltage measured at inner two probes. Eliminates contact resistance error (3–4 orders of magnitude smaller).

Golden Rule of Experimental Skills: For EVERY measurement in EVERY experiment: (1) Understand HOW the instrument makes its measurement, (2) Identify WHERE the sensor is located, (3) Find possible sources of error, (4) Compensate and correct for those errors, (5) Cross-check with independent methods, (6) Feel confident to DEFEND your measurements.

Week 2

Literature Survey — Search, Organize, and Reference

📚 What is a Literature Survey?

DISCLAIMER: Literature survey is NOT just Googling. Google looks at private websites. Literature survey = survey of technical, authoritative literature from universities, research labs, and scientific enterprises. Google Scholar is the closest Google equivalent.

Why Do a Literature Survey?

  • Know what approaches others have used and where gaps exist
  • Avoid duplication — even unintentional duplication is unacceptable in research degrees
  • Clarify controversial results
  • Establish state-of-the-art and build on others' work
  • Place your study in a current context where peers see it as valuable
  • Establish context and background for your own work
  • Point out anomalies and gaps
Novel technique + familiar area

A new method applied to a well-known problem = valid contribution

Familiar technique + novel area

A known method applied to a brand new problem = also valid

Key Exam Point: Literature review must be carried out throughout the research process — NOT only at the start, NOT only at the end, NOT only while publishing.

🔍 Search Strategies — The Three Methods

1. Keyword Search

Use specific words, author names, or article-defined keywords. Cast a wide net. Tools: Google Scholar, ScienceDirect, SpringerLink, INSPEC, PubMed.

2. Backward Chronological

Start with a recent, relevant article → examine its reference list → trace back to foundational papers → understand historical development.

3. Forward Chronological Search: Start with a key, foundational paper → find ALL papers that have cited it using citation indexes (Web of Science, Scopus) → trace how idea evolved → identify current state-of-the-art. This is the most powerful method for tracking field evolution.
Exam Point: Which strategy traces how an idea has evolved over time? = Forward chronological search using citation databases like Web of Science and Scopus.

Reference Manager Software

  • Zotero: Free, browser integration, auto-imports from websites
  • Mendeley: Social networking features, PDF management
  • All automate bibliography formatting and allow switching between citation styles (APA, MLA, Chicago, etc.)

  • Google Scholar: Broad search; not limited to subscribed journals

Best Practice: Start using a reference manager from Day 1 of your research. Retrospective bibliography management is painful and error-prone.

Week 3

Data Analysis — From Raw Data to Meaningful Conclusions

📊 What is Data Analysis?

Data analysis is far more than feeding numbers into software. It is the process of extracting useful, relevant, and meaningful information from observations. The goal: understand what data means, how certain we are, and how to make decisions.

Type of AnalysisDescriptionExample
ExploratoryMaking initial sense of data — searching for patterns, trends, outliersHistogram analysis, scatter plots
ConfirmatoryUsing data to confirm or reject a pre-existing hypothesisTesting if average salary in an age group = S₀
PredictiveForecasting future values based on patternsForecasting stock prices
PrescriptiveDiagnosing why an event occurred and suggesting remediesRoot cause analysis
Exam Trap: "Testing that the average salary in an age group is S₀" = CONFIRMATORY, not exploratory.

⚙️ Deterministic vs Stochastic Data

The most critical assumption in data analysis is about the Data Generating Process (DGP). This assumption determines ALL subsequent choices of tools and interpretations.

Deterministic DGP

DGP is perfectly known. Mathematical function perfectly explains the data. No uncertainty in observed values. Example: Ball dropped in still air — physics predicts time perfectly.

Stochastic DGP

Observations are a subset of many possibilities. No mathematical function perfectly explains the data. Observations have uncertainties. Example: Sensor noise, stock market, weather. Stochasticity ≠ zero predictability.

Critical Point: In practice, NO process is truly deterministic since perfect knowledge is never available. We say a process is predominantly deterministic or stochastic. Many processes are a mix (e.g., well-understood reactor + noisy sensor). Determinism/stochasticity rests with the knowledge of the analyst.
Key Insight: Any estimate inferred from stochastic data ALSO has uncertainty in it. Therefore, always compute and report the error in your estimate. Choose methods where error shrinks as sample size N → ∞.

📐 Key Terminology in Data Analysis

TermDefinitionKey Distinction
PopulationALL events or possibilities that could ever be observedTrue; never fully accessible
SampleSubset of the population actually obtainedWhat you actually have; used to infer population
Truth (θ₀)Actual parameter for the entire populationUnknown; e.g., true average temperature
Estimate (θ̂)Value inferred from the samplee.g., sample mean; NOT the truth
StatisticAny mathematical function of observationse.g., sample mean, sample variance
EstimatorMathematical function devised to estimate an unknown quantityAll estimators are statistics; not all statistics are estimators
Ensemble AverageAverage across ALL possible data records at a single time pointvs. Sample average: over one data record

Bias vs Variability

Bias (Accuracy)

Bias(θ̂) = E(θ̂) − θ₀. Zero bias = unbiased = high accuracy. Systematic error. If expected value of estimate equals truth → unbiased.

Variability (Precision)

Spread of estimates across different samples. Var(θ̂) = E(θ̂ − E(θ̂))². Low variability = high precision. High precision instrument = low variability.

Efficiency: Most efficient estimator = lowest variability/highest precision among all estimators. The most efficient estimate is NOT necessarily the most accurate — they measure different things.
Ideal Goal: Zero bias (accurate) AND low variability (precise). In practice, both may not be achievable from finite samples.

📋 Systematic Procedure for Data Analysis

Key Principle: Data analysis exercises are iterative, NOT necessarily sequential. Success depends on: (1) quality of data and (2) methodology. The 80/20 rule applies: expect 80% of time on data preparation.
  1. Preliminary Questions & Scoping: What type of analysis? (exploratory/confirmatory/predictive/prescriptive). Where does data come from? How was it acquired? What assumptions on DGP? (deterministic/stochastic/linear/non-linear/stationary) — This last question is the MOST important.
  2. Data Preparation & Pre-processing: Visualize first — ALWAYS plot before any calculation (Anscombe's Quartet lesson!). Check quality: outliers, missing values, noise. Set aside data for cross-validation (training vs test sets).
  3. Core Analysis & Modelling: Choose domain (time/frequency). Choose right tool: sample mean (best for Gaussian, no outliers) vs sample median (robust to outliers). Apply domain knowledge at every step.
  4. Assessment & Reporting: NEVER report estimates without error bars/confidence intervals. Subject parameters to hypothesis tests. Cross-validate. Use AIC/BIC if multiple models fit. Seek "best working model" not "right model."

📉 Anscombe's Quartet — Why Visualization is Essential

Four datasets with identical statistical properties (mean, variance, correlation, regression line) but completely different visual features:

DatasetPattern
Dataset 1Standard linear relationship
Dataset 2Parabolic (non-linear) — clearly not linear from picture
Dataset 3Perfect linear relationship with ONE outlier distorting everything
Dataset 4All x-values same except one extreme outlier — no relationship except one point's influence
Lesson: Based on descriptive statistics alone you CANNOT distinguish these datasets. Only visualization reveals the truth. Always plot your data before, during, and after analysis.

🧮 Modelling, Overfitting, Underfitting

AspectUnderfittingCorrect FitOverfitting
Model complexityToo simpleRight number of paramsToo complex (too many params)
Training performancePoorGoodExcellent/perfect
Test performancePoorGoodPOOR (fails on fresh data)
Food Analogy: "Data is the food for identification and parameters are guests." Over-invite guests (overfit) → some go hungry (large parameter errors). Under-invite (underfit) → not enough guests to eat the food.
Two Symptoms of Overfitting: (1) Large errors in parameter estimates — insignificant estimates for truly non-zero parameters. (2) Predictions fail on fresh data — poor cross-validation performance.

Adjusted R² = 1 − (MS_Error/MS_Total). Penalizes for extra parameters. If Adj R² decreases when you add a term → that term is useless. Don't chase R² = 1.

p-value rule: "If the p-value is low, the null hypothesis must go" — famous quote from Ogunnaike's book. If p-value is extremely low → reject null hypothesis → parameter is significant.

📋 Week 3 — Master Summary

  • Data analysis is iterative — not necessarily sequential
  • 80/20 rule: 80% time on data preparation
  • Most important first question: What is the DGP? (deterministic vs stochastic)
  • Stochastic ≠ unpredictable; just acknowledges incomplete knowledge
  • Always visualize FIRST — numbers alone deceive (Anscombe's Quartet)
  • Bias = accuracy; Variability = precision — they are independent
  • Always report error bars — never report estimates without uncertainty
  • Sample mean = best when Gaussian, no outliers; Sample median = robust to outliers
  • Overfitting: Too many parameters → fails on test data → Adj R² goes down
  • Confirmatory ≠ exploratory; testing specific value = confirmatory
  • Best working model ≠ the "right" model — use AIC/BIC for model selection

Week 4

Technical Writing, Scientific Presentations & Lab Safety

✍️ Technical Writing Fundamentals

A mystery novel succeeds by hiding the truth until the final page. A scientific paper succeeds by revealing it in the first paragraph. This is the single most counter-intuitive shift for new writers.

Core Principle: EFFICIENCY — Every element from sentence structure to illustration choice is designed to help the reader understand the work as quickly and clearly as possible. The goal is NOT to force someone to read the whole paper, but to deliver key information effectively.

Anatomy of a Journal Article

SectionPurposeKey Exam Facts
TitleIdentifies broad research area AND specific novel contributionMust contain both elements — NOT just broad topic
AbstractComplete standalone summary — gives away ALL major findings upfrontNo undefined abbreviations. Written for readers who may never read the rest. "Elevator pitch."
IntroductionWhat? Why important? What's needed to understand?Compare with prior work to establish novelty: "X did this, Y did that, but no one has explored..."
Methods/ExperimentalThe "recipe" — enough detail to reproduce the experimentNothing hidden. Another researcher must be able to repeat.
ResultsFactual, objective statements of data ONLY — no interpretationA technician can write results. Non-controversial. "Hardness increased with decreasing grain size."
DiscussionThe "so what?" — interpretation, analysis, scientific argument about WHYCan be debated. Different experts may have different discussions of same results.
ConclusionSummarizes key findings for readers who have read the paperMore technical freedom than abstract. May include future perspectives.
Results vs Discussion — Most Important Distinction:
Results = WHAT you observed (factual, non-controversial, any technician would state the same).
Discussion = WHY you observed it (interpretation, can be debated by different experts).

Language & Illustration Principles

  • Keep sentences SHORT, direct, simple — if you must reread a sentence to understand it, it's too long
  • Avoid flowery language, arrogant phrases ("it is obvious that...", "clearly...")
  • Illustrations: Graphs for trends, Tables for specific values, Schematics for complex objects, Photographs for realism (watch out for distracting details)
  • A technical paper and a science fiction novel have very different styles — novel hides truth, paper reveals it upfront
  • Purpose of technical writing: Inform, Persuade, Convey ideas efficiently — NOT sarcasm

🎤 Scientific Presentations

A presentation is different from a paper in important ways:

Paper

One-time read. Peer reviewed. Dense with information. Reader controls pace. Can re-read. Permanent record.

Presentation

Interactive with audience. Changes based on audience. NOT peer reviewed. NOT a one-time activity (can be given multiple times). Audience controls pace.

Key Principles for Presentations

  • Know your audience — gauge their background, tailor content accordingly, understand why they're watching
  • Outline at start — helps audience see main topics AND identify which sections interest them
  • One idea per slide — illustrations must help audience focus on a SPECIFIC idea
  • Open spaces — don't fill slides end to end; sufficient white space helps focus
  • Don't read slides — you're a scientist, not a presenter reading slides
  • Slides must NOT be complete text — they complement speech, not replace it
  • Color is NOT mandatory — monochrome is fine if clearer
Exam Points:
Presentations: (A) change based on audience ✓ (D) generally interactive ✓ — NOT one-time, NOT peer reviewed.
Outline helps audience: (B) note main points ✓ (C) decide which sections interest them ✓.
Knowing audience: (B) gauge background ✓ (C) tailor talk ✓ (D) understand their motivation ✓.

⚠️ Laboratory Safety

Safety is CULTURE — not just procedures. In research labs, we push boundaries with new experiments, making active involvement in safety even more critical than in industry.

Common Lab Hazards

Five common lab hazards: Chemicals, Electricity, Water, Gas Bottles, Heat Sources (hot plates, ovens, furnaces up to 1500°C).

PPE (Personal Protective Equipment)

  • Lab Coat: Wear the moment you enter the lab. Not just for acid — for ANY spill.
  • Gloves: Nitrile (general), acid-resistant (acids), thermal (hot items). Check glove-solvent compatibility.
  • Safety Goggles: Must provide COMPLETE coverage (sides, top, bottom) AND be shatter-proof. Regular prescription glasses are NOT adequate — don't cover sides and can shatter. Safety goggles fit OVER prescription glasses.
  • Dust Masks: Surgical masks for basic dust; elaborate filter masks for chemical dust. Check adequacy for your specific samples.

Chemical Safety (Acids)

Exam Point — Acid Safety (A, B, D are correct; C is WRONG):
✅ A. Acid storage must be done with secondary containment.
✅ B. Acids and bases must be separated.
❌ C. Acid wastes must be washed down the sink — THIS IS WRONG. Acid wastes must NOT be washed down the sink.
✅ D. Acid-containing bottles must be clearly labelled.

Gas Bottle Safety

  • Gas bottles are pressurized units — if neck breaks, uncontrolled gas escape is very dangerous
  • Must be secured with a chain attached to the wall — chain must be at about ¾ height to prevent toppling
  • Chain too low → bottle can still fall → safety purpose NOT served
  • Transport: Use dedicated trolleys with chain arrangements. Do NOT roll on the floor.
  • Store with regulators REMOVED — regulators only attached when in use
  • NEVER have a gas bottle with regulator installed that isn't secured

🌍 TRIPS Agreement & History

  • TRIPS = Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights
  • Agreement under the World Trade Organization (WTO)
  • Mandates 20-year patent term for all WTO member countries
  • Technology agnostic — applies to ALL fields of technology
  • Before TRIPS: Patent term was 14 years in most countries
  • In India before TRIPS: 14 years general, up to 7 years for pharmaceuticals

Why 14 Years Historically?

In England: 7 years to train an apprentice. Craftsmen were given exclusive privilege in exchange for training 2 British apprentices. 7 + 7 = 14 years.

Historical Context

  • IPR attained international recognition with advent of Industrial Revolution (NOT Green Revolution!)
  • Advancement in printing technology → quick spread of ideas → mass duplication possible
  • Before Industrial Revolution: Oral-formulaic tradition (memorize and pass on) — Shakespeare benefited from this
  • Copyright requires things to be recorded/expressed on some medium — memorizing is NOT "making a copy"

Patent Specification & Claims

  • Patent specification = descriptive document for an invention (like a land deed)
  • Contains: background art, description, embodiments, illustrations, and Claims
  • Claims = concluding part that demarcates the legal boundaries of the IP
  • Protection is confined to WHAT IS MENTIONED IN THE CLAIMS — NOT the descriptive part
  • Prior art = what has gone before (existing technology). Inventor must distinguish from prior art.
  • Patent prosecution = examination by patent office

Copyright — Idea/Expression Dichotomy

Copyright protects the expression of an idea, NOT the idea itself. Many authors can write "whodunit" crime novels (the idea), but each unique text is a protected expression. Agatha Christie could write dozens of novels with the same detective — each receives its own copyright because the specific text is unique.

Week 7

Design of Experiments (DOE) — Statistics, ANOVA, Regression & Factorial Design

📐 The Fundamental DOE Equation

yᵢ = ηᵢ + εᵢ yᵢ → actual measurement recorded (what you write down) ηᵢ → TRUE value (unknown; what you really want to know) εᵢ → random error (positive or negative; scatter is unavoidable; NOT your mistake)
Key Insight: You can NEVER directly know ηᵢ. Experiments give estimates. The scatter εᵢ is not a mistake — it's physics. DOE teaches you to live with scatter and still draw powerful conclusions.

Why do experiments? (a) To prove theory, (b) Proof of concept, (c) When theory is too complex to develop analytically.

📊 Probability & Distributions

Probability Density Function (PDF)

  • Random Variable X — abstract before experiment; becomes small x after measurement
  • PDF f(x) — describes distribution of probabilities for a continuous random variable
  • Total area under the curve = 1 (always, for any valid PDF)
  • P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx = F(b) − F(a)
  • ∫ₐᵃ f(x) dx = 0 always (single point has zero probability)
Mean (μ): E[X] = ∫₋∞^∞ x · f(x) dx Variance (σ²): E[(X−μ)²] = ∫₋∞^∞ (x−μ)² · f(x) dx
Exam Trap: "∫₀^∞ f(x)dx = 1" is NOT always true — the total area from -∞ to +∞ = 1. If distribution is only on positive values (like chi-square), then ∫₀^∞ = 1, but NOT for all distributions.

Normal Distribution — The Core of DOE

PropertyValue/Formula
NotationN(μ, σ²) — parameters are mean μ and variance σ²
PDFf(x) = (1/√(2πσ²)) · exp(−(x−μ)²/(2σ²))
ShapeSymmetric, bell-shaped, unimodal
SymmetrySymmetric about the MEAN (not SD, not SD×Mean)
Mean = Median = ModeAll three are equal for normal distribution
68–95–99.7 Rule: ±1σ from mean → ~68% of data ±2σ from mean → ~95% of data ±3σ from mean → ~99.7% of data
Standardization: If X ~ N(μ, σ²), then Z = (X − μ)/σ ~ N(0,1). Use a SINGLE Z-table for ANY normal distribution!

📈 Sampling & Estimation

ConceptPopulationSample
Meanμ (true, unknown)x̄ = Σxᵢ/n (estimator)
Varianceσ² (true, unknown)s² = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²/(n−1) (estimator)
TypeParameterStatistic / Point Estimate
Why n−1 in sample variance? Degrees of freedom. Among n deviations (xᵢ−x̄), they must sum to 0 — so only n−1 are independent. Dividing by n−1 gives UNBIASED estimate of σ².

Sampling Distribution of the Mean

E[x̄] = μ → mean of sample means = population mean Var[x̄] = σ²/n → variance of sample means = σ²/n SD[x̄] = σ/√n → standard error of the mean
Intuition: Larger sample → smaller variance of means → more precise estimates. Sample of size 100 = 10× more precise than size 1 (in terms of SD).

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

CLT: Even if the parent population is NOT normal, the sampling distribution of the mean approaches normality as n increases. n > 30 is the rule of thumb.
FALSE statements about CLT (exam traps):
❌ "If parent is normal, sampling distribution is NOT normal for n<30" — FALSE. If parent IS normal, sampling dist IS always normal regardless of n.
❌ "If parent is not normal, sampling dist is also not normal regardless of n" — FALSE. CLT fixes this for large n (>30).

📏 Confidence Intervals & Hypothesis Testing

Confidence Intervals

100(1−α)% CI when σ is known: x̄ − z(α/2)·σ/√n ≤ μ ≤ x̄ + z(α/2)·σ/√n α = significance level (0.05 for 95% CI) z(α/2) ≈ 1.96 for 95% CI (α = 0.05)
Meaning: If you take 100 random samples and compute 95% CIs from each, ~95 of those intervals will contain the true μ. Note: μ is fixed; the interval varies across samples.

t-distribution: When σ² is unknown AND sample size is small, use t = (x̄−μ)/(s/√n) with n−1 degrees of freedom. As df → ∞, t-distribution → Normal.

Chi-Square & F Distributions

Chi-Square (χ²)

χ² = (n−1)s²/σ² with n−1 df. Used for CIs on variance. Only positive values (right-skewed). NOT symmetric.

F Distribution

Ratio of two chi-squares scaled by their DoF. Two sets of DoF: numerator (m₁) and denominator (m₂). Used in ANOVA.

Hypothesis Testing

Decision \ RealityH₀ is TRUEH₀ is FALSE
Do NOT reject H₀✅ CorrectType II Error (β) — missed detection
Reject H₀Type I Error (α) ← MORE SERIOUS✅ Correct
Type I Error > Type II Error in severity:
Type I = Innocent person convicted = discarding a perfectly good process for an unproven one.
Type II = Guilty person freed = keeping a bad process when a good one exists.
We control α (Type I error rate) when setting significance level.

Scope of hypothesis testing: concerns parameters of probability distribution of the population, not the sample. It relies on data from the sample.

Interaction Effects

  • Parallel lines in interaction plot → NO interaction between factors
  • Crossing, converging, or diverging lines → Interaction EXISTS
  • Interaction means: Factor A's effect on response DEPENDS on the level of Factor B

Randomization in Experiments

Randomize run order! Uncontrolled lurking effects (temperature build-up, instrument drift, operator fatigue) get evenly distributed across all treatments rather than systematically biasing one. Example: Testing 3 toners sequentially — copier heats up and Toner C always gets hotter conditions. Randomization prevents this bias.

🔢 Factorial Design & Regression

2ⁿ Factorial Design

  • n factors, each at 2 levels (Low and High). Total runs = 2ⁿ
  • Economical — maximum information from minimum runs vs one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT)
  • Captures interactions — OFAT misses interaction effects completely
  • Both qualitative and quantitative factors can be analyzed together

Linear Regression Model

y = β₀ + β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + ... + βₖXₖ + ε β₀ → intercept β₁...βₖ → partial regression coefficients (k+1 unknowns total) ε → random error ~ N(0, σ²) Matrix form: y = Xβ + ε Least-squares solution: β̂ = (X'X)⁻¹ X'y
Coefficient of Determination: R² = SS_Regression / SS_Total = 1 − SS_Error/SS_Total R² = 1 when SS_Error = 0 (perfect fit) ← equivalent to SS_Regression = SS_Total Adjusted R² = 1 − (MS_Error/MS_Total) ← penalizes for extra parameters
Don't chase R² = 1! Adding useless parameters always inflates R². Use Adjusted R² — if Adj R² decreases when you add a term, that term is useless.

Response Surface Methodology (RSM)

  • RSM finds the direction of steepest ascent/descent to reach the optimum efficiently
  • Central Composite Design (CCD): 2ⁿ factorial + 2k axial points + center points
  • Center points detect curvature; axial points estimate individual quadratic terms

Canonical Analysis (Eigenvalues of B matrix)

All eigenvalues NEGATIVE → stationary point is a MAXIMUM
All eigenvalues POSITIVE → stationary point is a MINIMUM
Mixed signs → SADDLE POINT

Success = correct answerSuccess = novel contribution + defensible methodology

Primary Objectives of Research

  • (1) Acquiring new knowledge
  • (2) Staying updated with latest advances
  • (3) Systematic examination and critical analysis
  • ALL of the above — the correct NPTEL exam answer

Starting Point for Research

Primary requirement when starting research: Establishing a research question. NOT starting data analysis, NOT writing the thesis first, NOT publishing immediately.

🔗 Interdisciplinary Research

Primary objective of interdisciplinary research: To incorporate a comprehensive methodology by merging two or more fields. NOT to complicate problems, NOT to diminish individual field focus.

India-Scale Thinking

Not all critical problems need the newest tools. Innovative application of existing methods to strategic problems (DRDO, ISRO, rural development) is equally valuable and important.

🎓 Research Scholar's Journey

The big challenge: Transitioning from structured coursework to open-ended research. Students accustomed to "given problem, find answer" must shift to "define the problem, justify your assumptions."

  • Approach it as a positive challenge, not an obstacle
  • Embrace interdisciplinary work — the world doesn't fit into vertical silos
  • Advisor meetings: Weekly initially; frequency adjusts as independence grows. This is a two-person agreement.
  • Peer group discussion is as important as meeting the guide

🧠 Week 8 Quiz — NPTEL PYQs

Q1. Which of the following processes are required in experimental research? MULTI

A. Controlling variables
B. Manipulating results to support the hypothesis
C. Making observations
D. Collecting references
✅ A, C, D. Controlling variables, making observations, and collecting references are all required. B — manipulating results — is NOT required and is in fact unethical. This is a key NPTEL exam trap.

Q2. Why is manipulation of results discouraged in research? MULTI

A. It distorts the validity and reliability of findings
B. It undermines the integrity and credibility of the researcher
C. It hampers the progress of knowledge
D. It makes publishing easier
✅ A, B, C — All of the above (except D). In NPTEL format, the answer is often "all of the above" for this question. Manipulation has all three negative consequences.

Q3. What is the primary requirement when starting research?

A. Begin data analysis immediately
B. Write the thesis draft
C. Establishing a research question
D. Publishing preliminary findings
✅ C — Establishing a research question. Before anything else, you must define what you are trying to answer. Without a clear research question, all subsequent work lacks direction.

Q4. What is the primary objective of interdisciplinary research?

A. To complicate the research problems
B. To diminish the importance of any single field
C. To incorporate a comprehensive methodology by merging two or more fields
D. To limit the scope of research to one discipline
✅ C. Interdisciplinary research merges fields for comprehensive methodology. Modern challenges rarely fit into single discipline "silos." A, B, D are all contradictions of the actual purpose.

Q5. The success of a PhD student can be measured by: MULTI

A. Clear understanding and ability to explain their work to others
B. Identification of limitations and boundaries of their research
C. Ability to carry out research independently
D. Continued dependence on the advisor for all decisions
✅ A, B, C. All three are indicators of PhD success. D is the OPPOSITE — continued dependence on the advisor is a sign of failure, not success. The goal is to become an independent researcher.

Q6. What is the definition of research?

A. The act of repeating a search process
B. Just discovering solutions to known problems
C. Conducting systematic scientific investigations to uncover the truth about a specific problem
D. Publishing as many papers as possible
✅ C. Research = systematic scientific investigations to uncover truth. A is a common wrong answer (pun on "re-search" = search again). B is too narrow. D is a caricature of research culture, not its definition.
