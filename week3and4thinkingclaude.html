📊 WEEK 3 — Data Analysis & Modelling Skills

1. What is Data Analysis?

Definition: Data analysis is the process of extracting useful, relevant, and meaningful information from observations in a systematic manner.

Data analysis is not simply throwing data into software and reporting fancy graphs. It is a theory-grounded discipline requiring rigorous methodology, domain knowledge, and careful interpretation at every step.

Purposes of Data Analysis

  • Parameter Estimation — Inferring unknowns (means, variances, model coefficients, etc.)
  • Model Development & Prediction — Building relationships between variables; forecasting
  • Feature Extraction & Classification — Identifying patterns (e.g., peaks in ECG), classifying images
  • Hypothesis Testing — Verifying postulates with statistical rigor (e.g., "Is average temperature 25°C?")
  • Fault Detection / Process Monitoring — Comparing current data against a normal template to detect anomalies

Where Does Analysis Fit In?

Data analysis sits between raw observations and actionable applications. The flow is:

Process → Sensors → Data → [Visualization → Quality Check → Pre-processing] → [Descriptive Analysis → Confirmatory Analysis → Estimation → Modelling] → [Monitoring → Control → Optimization → Innovation]

Domain knowledge should be factored in at every stage of this pipeline.

3. Deterministic vs Stochastic Data

The most critical assumption in data analysis is about the Data Generating Process (DGP). This assumption determines the entire theoretical framework, the tools used, and how results are interpreted.

Deterministic DGP
The DGP is perfectly known OR a mathematical function can perfectly explain the data OR there is no uncertainty in observed values OR the process is accurately predictable. Example: A ball dropped in still air — physics predicts time to hit the floor perfectly.
Stochastic DGP
Observations are only a subset of many (finite or infinite) possibilities OR no mathematical function can perfectly explain the data OR observations have uncertainties. Example: Sensor noise, stock market index, weather prediction. Stochasticity does NOT mean zero predictability.
⚠️ Critical Point: In practice, NO process is truly deterministic since perfect knowledge is never available. We say a process is predominantly deterministic or predominantly stochastic. Many engineering processes are a mix of both (e.g., well-understood reactor + noisy sensor).

Why Does This Assumption Matter?

  • The theory, tools, and interpretations of results heavily depend on whether you assume determinism or stochasticity
  • Definitions of periodicity, mean, variance, stationarity differ between deterministic and stochastic signals
  • The sample mean is NOT the true mean — it is only an estimate of the true mean
  • Assuming stochasticity is a formal way of saying "I do not have sufficient understanding of the process"

When to Assume Which?

  • If physical process is well-understood and errors are negligible → predominantly deterministic
  • If causes are unknown or mechanism is too complex → assume stochastic
  • If part is well-understood and part is not (e.g., reactor + noisy sensor) → mixed
  • Determinism/stochasticity rests with the knowledge of the analyst — there is no perfect formula to determine it a priori
⚡ Key Insight: Any estimate inferred from data with uncertainties ALSO has uncertainty in it! Therefore, always compute and report the error in your estimate. Choose estimation methods where error shrinks as sample size N → ∞.

4. Key Terminology in Data Analysis

Population vs Sample
Population (Ensemble): The collection of ALL events or possibilities that could ever be observed. Sample: The subset of the population actually obtained through sampling (one data record). We use the sample to make inferences about the unobservable population.
Truth vs Estimate
Truth: The actual population parameter (e.g., true average temperature). Estimate: The value inferred from the sample (e.g., sample mean). Estimates will always differ from truth because we haven't seen all possibilities.
Statistic vs Estimator
Statistic: Any mathematical function of observations (e.g., sample mean, sample variance). Estimator: A mathematical function of observations devised specifically for the purpose of estimating an unknown quantity. Both are mathematical functions of data — the difference is purpose. Sample mean is both a statistic AND an estimator of the true mean.
Ensemble Average vs Sample Average
Ensemble average (Expectation): Average computed across ALL possible data records at a single point in time. Sample average: Average computed over all observations for ONE data record.
Bias (Accuracy)
Systematic error in an estimate. Bias(θ̂) = E(θ̂) − θ₀. If the expected value of the estimate equals the truth, the estimator is unbiased (zero bias = high accuracy). Low bias = high accuracy.
Variability (Precision)
Spread of estimates across different data records. Var(θ̂) = E(θ̂ − E(θ̂))². Low variability = high precision. If you repeatedly measure the same variable and get consistent results, the estimator is precise. This is what "high precision instrument" means.
Efficiency
An estimator is most efficient when it estimates parameters with the lowest variability / highest precision — i.e., the lowest standard error among all estimators of that parameter.
Ideal Goal: We want estimates with zero bias (accurate) and low variability (precise). In practice, both may not be achievable from finite samples. The most efficient estimate is not necessarily the most accurate — they measure different things.

5. Systematic Procedure for Data Analysis

Key Principle: Almost all data analysis exercises are iterative, not necessarily sequential. Success depends on TWO factors: quality of data and methodology of data analysis. The "80/20 rule" applies: expect to spend 80% of time on data preparation.
1

Preliminary Questions & Scoping

What type of analysis is required? (exploratory, confirmatory, predictive, prescriptive) • Where does data come from? (domain) • How was data acquired? (sampling mechanism, hardware) • How informative is the data? (quality, outliers, missing values, noise) • What assumptions on the DGP? (deterministic/stochastic, linear/non-linear, stationary/non-stationary) — This last question is perhaps the MOST important.

2

Data Preparation & Pre-processing

Visualize First, Always: Plot data BEFORE any calculation. Numbers alone can deceive (Anscombe's Quartet). Quality Check: Identify outliers, missing values, noise. Pre-processing: Clean up outliers OR use robust methods; fill missing data; synchronize timestamps; filter noise; data reconciliation. Partition Data: Always set aside data for cross-validation (training vs test sets).

3

Core Analysis & Modelling

Choose Domain: Time domain, frequency domain (Fourier transform for periodicity), joint time-frequency (wavelets). Dimensionality Reduction: PCA, NMF for large-dimensional data. Choose Right Tool: Sample mean (efficient if Gaussian, no outliers) vs sample median (robust to outliers). In hypothesis tests, choose appropriate statistic and significance level. In modelling, choose appropriate regressor set and model structure. Factor in Domain Knowledge at every step (e.g., don't build linear models for a pH system you know is non-linear).

4

Assessment & Reporting

Quantify Uncertainty: Never report estimates without error bars/confidence intervals. Subject parameters to hypothesis tests for statistical significance. Cross-validate models on test data. If multiple models fit, use information-theoretic measures (AIC, BIC) and end-use criteria. We seek the best working model, not necessarily the "right" model. Any report should include estimates, error bounds, and validation test results.

💡 Remember: Data analysis is both a science (objective statistical methods) and an art (subjective judgment, domain expertise). There may not be a unique answer at every stage — and that's normal.

6. Anscombe's Quartet & The Power of Visualization

Anscombe's Quartet is four datasets that have identical statistical properties (mean, variance, correlation, regression line) but completely different visual features:

  • Dataset 1: Standard linear relationship
  • Dataset 2: Parabolic (non-linear) relationship — clearly not linear
  • Dataset 3: Perfect linear relationship with ONE outlier
  • Dataset 4: All x-values identical except one extreme outlier — no relationship except influence of one point
⚠️ Lesson: Based on descriptive statistics alone, you CANNOT distinguish these four datasets. Only visualization reveals the massive differences. This powerfully demonstrates why you must always plot your data before, during, and after analysis. Outliers can completely distort your results.

Visualization Tools

Trend plots, scatter plots, bar charts, box plots, histograms, color maps, contour plots, and specialized plots. Convert numbers to pictures at every stage.

7. Modelling Skills & Overfitting

Linear Regression in Practice

Using the lm() function in R for fitting linear models (least squares estimation). The summary() function provides: coefficient estimates, standard errors, t-values, p-values, and significance stars.

Interpreting Model Output

  • Estimates: By themselves don't carry much meaning — must be interpreted relative to standard error
  • Standard Error: If much smaller than the estimate → parameter is significant
  • p-value: If extremely low → reject null hypothesis that parameter is zero → parameter is significant
  • "If the p-value is low, the null hypothesis must go" — memorable phrase from Ogunnaike's book
  • Stars (*** ** *): Indicate significance level

Input Excitation & Unique Parameter Estimation

Input excitation can greatly influence the ability to estimate parameters uniquely. If the input does not sufficiently "excite" the system, parameter estimates may be unreliable or non-unique.

Overfitting vs Underfitting

AspectUnderfittingCorrect FitOverfitting
Model complexityToo simple (fewer parameters than needed)Right number of parametersToo complex (more parameters than needed)
Training performancePoorGoodExcellent (may be perfect)
Test/prediction performancePoorGoodPoor (fails on fresh data)
Parameter estimatesSignificant but wrong modelSignificant and close to truthLarge errors; some parameters become insignificant even if truly non-zero
"Data is the food for identification and parameters are guests." When you don't have enough food (data) for your guests (parameters), some guests go hungry (large errors). We don't want to over-invite (overfit) or under-invite (underfit). Invite exactly the number of guests the food can support.
⚡ Two Symptoms of Overfitting:
1. Large errors in parameter estimates (insignificant estimates for truly non-zero parameters)
2. Predictions fail on fresh data (poor cross-validation performance)
Overfitting does NOT mean more time spent fitting. Underfitted models are less complex than overfitted models.

Functions Linear in Time t

  • A sinusoidal signal y(t) = A sin(ω₀t) is NOT linear in t
  • x(t) = t·exp(c₀t) + β when c₀ = 0 becomes x(t) = t + β → linear in t ✓
  • Distance = velocity × time for uniform velocity → linear in t ✓
  • Instantaneous frequency of a linear chirp x(t) = cos(2π(αt + f₀)t) is f(t) = 2αt + f₀ → linear in t ✓

8. Technical Presentations

Journal Paper vs Technical Presentation

FeatureJournal PaperTechnical Presentation
NatureOne-time activity w.r.t. a given datasetAn event; can be repeated with modifications
StructureFormal, rigid; follows journal constraintsSignificant flexibility; changes based on audience
Peer ReviewYes — evaluated by independent anonymous expertsGenerally not peer reviewed
InteractivityNot interactive; reader reads independentlyGenerally interactive; audience asks questions
Audience ControlNo control — anyone can read itSpecific audience on each occasion

Two Key Constraints

1. Audience

  • What is their background? — Pitch the talk at the right level (school students vs conference vs funding agency)
  • Why are they watching? — Funding agency: highlight uniqueness; Conference: highlight new results vs prior work; School: highlight exciting general concepts
  • You CANNOT present the same talk everywhere — customize for each audience

2. Duration

  • Keeping track of time shows you value audience's and your own time
  • Rule of thumb: ~1 slide per minute (for 50 min talk → ~35 slides)
  • You should be able to present the same content in 5 min, 20 min, or 1 hour by adjusting detail level
  • Never flash all slides quickly if time is cut — choose key slides and present them well
  • Quantity of information ≠ audience's ability to absorb it

Connecting with Your Audience — Do's and Don'ts

❌ DON'T✅ DO
Overfill slides from end to endUse sufficient open space; one specific idea per slide
Overuse special effects / animationsUse special effects judiciously to highlight specific points
Write everything on slide and read from itUse bullet points as prompts; elaborate conversationally
Turn your back to audience and read the screenFace the audience; look at their eyes; gauge understanding
Use multiple fonts randomlyStick to 1-2 standard fonts throughout

Outline Slide Best Practice

Provide an outline slide at the beginning. Flash it at several points during the talk so the audience knows where they are. It helps them note the main points you'll cover and decide which sections interest them most.

Illustrations in Presentations

  • Choose the right type: Tables for specific values; Graphs for trends/maxima; Photographs for realism; Drawings/Schematics for highlighting specific features without distraction
  • Pay attention to details: Always include axis labels, units, values, legends, captions
  • Consistent significant digits in tables
  • Remove extraneous objects from photographs
  • Include scale bars — without scale, images are meaningless (e.g., Taj Mahal example; nanotube cross-section with 20nm scale bar)
  • Level of complexity: Sometimes a simple drawing conveys a point better than a cluttered photograph (e.g., Hubble telescope — schematic vs photo for showing solar panels)

9. Technical Writing & Journal Papers

Purpose of Technical Writing

The purpose is threefold: Inform the reader → Persuade the reader → Do both EFFICIENTLY. Efficiency is the single most important goal. Every element — sentence structure, illustration choice, document organization — is designed for maximum clarity and speed of understanding.

Why Technical Writing is Difficult

  • Familiarity with wrong examples: We grew up reading novels, newspapers, magazines — all have very different styles and purposes from technical papers
  • High expectations of first draft: Students expect the first draft to be perfect; in reality, the first 2-3 papers require many iterations
  • Not understanding why changes are requested: Without knowing what's expected, corrections feel arbitrary
⚠️ The Fatal Mistake: A mystery novel hides the truth until the final page. A scientific paper reveals it in the first paragraph (abstract). The style of a good novel is EXACTLY WRONG for a journal paper. In technical writing, you "give it all away" upfront. The purpose is NOT to force people to read the entire paper — it's to deliver information efficiently.

Why Publish? (Purpose)

  • Get formal credit for your research work
  • Inform the scientific community of new results
  • Convey ideas efficiently for others to build upon
  • Influence public policy and industrial innovation

The Publication Process

  1. Write paper according to journal constraints (style, length, format)
  2. Submit to a journal in your specific, narrow area of research
  3. Journal sends to independent anonymous expert reviewers (peer review)
  4. Receive reviewer comments; respond to comments
  5. If reviewers approve (work is new, significant, correct) → paper gets published

Anatomy of a Journal Article

Beginning

  • Title: Identifies the broad research area AND the specific novel contribution
  • Abstract: Complete standalone summary — gives away ALL major findings and conclusions upfront. Aimed at a reader who may never read the rest. No undefined abbreviations.
  • Introduction: What is your work? Why is it important? (Compare with prior published work to establish novelty: "Researcher X did this, Y did that, but no one has explored...") What is required to understand it?

Middle

  • Experimental Details / Methods: The "recipe" — detailed enough for another researcher to reproduce your experiment. Nothing hidden.
  • Results: Factual, objective statements of data (e.g., "hardness increased with decreasing grain size"). A result is something any technician can state — no judgment, no controversy.
  • Discussion: The "so what?" section — your interpretation, analysis, scientific argument about WHY you observed those results. This is where opinions and arguments come in, based on scientific principles. Different people can have different discussions about the same results.

Ending

  • Conclusion: Summarizes key findings (like abstract, but written for someone who has read the paper — more technical freedom). May include future perspectives.
  • References: List of all cited prior work

Language & Illustration in Technical Writing

  • Language: Keep sentences short, direct, simple. Avoid flowery language, arrogant phrases ("it is obvious that..."). If you must reread a sentence to understand it, it's too long.
  • Illustration: Choose wisely — graphs for trends, tables for specific values, schematics for simplifying complex objects, photographs for realism (but watch out for distracting details)

Key Differences: Results vs Discussion

ResultsDiscussion
Factual statement of dataInterpretation and analysis of data
Non-controversial — anyone would state the same thingCan be debated — different experts may have different opinions
"Hardness increased with decreasing grain size""This increase is attributed to the Hall-Petch relationship because..."

10. Laboratory Safety

Safety is an aspect of culture — it must be built into you, not just a set of procedures to follow. In research labs, we push boundaries with new experiments, making active involvement in safety even more critical than in industry (where activities are well-defined and repetitive).

Common Lab Hazards

Five things commonly found in labs that require formal safety attention: Chemicals, Electricity, Water, Gas Bottles, Heat Sources (hot plates, ovens, furnaces up to 1500°C).

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

  • Lab Coat: Wear the moment you enter the lab. Protects from spills.
  • Gloves: Nitrile gloves for general handling; acid-resistant gloves for acids; thermal gloves for hot items. Be aware of glove-solvent compatibility.
  • Safety Glasses/Goggles: Must provide COMPLETE coverage (sides, top, bottom) AND be shatter-proof. Regular prescription glasses are NOT adequate — they don't cover sides and can shatter. Safety goggles are sized to fit over prescription glasses.
  • Dust Masks: Surgical masks for basic dust; elaborate masks with filters for specific chemical dust. Check adequacy for your specific samples.

Chemical Safety (Acids)

  • Acid storage must be done with secondary containment
  • Acids and bases must be separated
  • Acid-containing bottles must be clearly labelled
  • Acid wastes must NOT be washed down the sink

Gas Bottle Safety

  • Gas bottles are pressurized units — if the neck breaks, gas escapes uncontrollably → very dangerous
  • Must be secured with a chain attached to the wall — chain must be high enough (about ¾ height) to prevent toppling
  • Chain too low → bottle can still fall → safety purpose NOT served
  • NEVER have a gas bottle with a regulator installed that isn't secured
  • For transport: Use dedicated trolleys with similar chain arrangements; do NOT roll on the floor
  • Store with regulators REMOVED (regulators are only attached when in use)

🎯 Master Summary — Weeks 3 & 4

📊 Data Analysis

Extracting useful, relevant, meaningful information systematically. Not just fancy graphs — requires theory, domain knowledge, and rigorous methodology. The 80/20 rule: 80% time on data prep.

🎲 DGP Assumption

Deterministic vs Stochastic is the MOST critical assumption. Determines theory, tools, and interpretations. In practice, no process is perfectly deterministic. Mixed cases common in engineering.

🎯 Bias vs Variability

Bias = systematic error = accuracy measure. Variability = spread of estimates = precision measure. Ideal: zero bias + low variability. Most efficient ≠ most accurate.

📈 Overfitting

Over-parameterized models: large errors in estimates + poor predictions on fresh data. "Data is food, parameters are guests." Always cross-validate. Underfit models are less complex than overfit ones.

👁️ Visualization

Always plot data BEFORE analysis. Anscombe's Quartet: identical statistics, wildly different features. Numbers deceive; pictures reveal. Visualize before, during, and after analysis.

📊 Reporting Results

NEVER report estimates without error bars/confidence intervals. Always conduct hypothesis tests. Cross-validate models. We seek the BEST WORKING model, not necessarily the RIGHT model.

🎤 Presentations

Customize for audience & duration. ~1 slide/minute. Outline slide shown multiple times. Don't overfill slides. Avoid reading from slides. Face the audience. Use illustrations wisely with proper labels, units, scale.

📝 Technical Writing

Purpose: inform + persuade EFFICIENTLY. Abstract gives it ALL away upfront (opposite of novels). Paper = Title → Abstract → Introduction → Methods → Results (facts) → Discussion (interpretation) → Conclusion. Peer reviewed. Simple, clear language.

🔬 Lab Safety

Safety is culture, not just procedures. Wear lab coat, gloves, safety goggles (NOT regular glasses). Gas bottles: chain at ¾ height, regulators removed for storage. Acids: secondary containment, separate from bases, clear labels, NO sink disposal.
