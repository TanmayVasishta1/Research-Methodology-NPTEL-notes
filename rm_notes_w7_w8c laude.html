Week 7 — Design of Experiments (DOE): Statistical Foundations

Probability, distributions, sampling, hypothesis testing, ANOVA, regression, and experimental design

Core Experiment Model

01The Fundamental DOE Equation

Every experimental measurement is modeled as:

yᵢ = ηᵢ + εᵢ yᵢ → actual measurement (what you record) ηᵢ → TRUE value (unknown, what you really want) εᵢ → random error (positive or negative — scatter is unavoidable)
Key insight: You can never know ηᵢ directly. Experiments give estimates. The scatter εᵢ is not your mistake — it's physics. DOE teaches you to live with scatter and still draw conclusions.

Why do experiments? (a) To prove theory, (b) Proof of concept, (c) When theory is too complex to develop analytically.

02Random Variables & Probability Density Function (PDF)

  • Random Variable X — abstract before experiment; becomes small x after measurement.
  • PDF f(x) — describes distribution of probabilities for a continuous random variable. Total area under the curve = 1.
  • CDF F(z) = ∫₋∞ᶻ f(x) dx — cumulative probability up to z.
P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx = F(b) − F(a)
Mean (μ): E[X] = ∫₋∞^∞ x·f(x) dx  |  Variance (σ²): E[(X−μ)²] = ∫₋∞^∞ (x−μ)²·f(x) dx
Exam trap: For a legitimate PDF: ∫₋∞^∞ f(x)dx = 1, NOT necessarily ∫₀^∞. Also, ∫ₐᵃ f(x)dx = 0 always (single point has zero probability).

03Normal Distribution — The Star of DOE

Parameters

Mean μ and Variance σ² are the parameters themselves (not derived from them)

Notation: N(μ, σ²)

PDF Form

f(x) = (1/√(2πσ²)) · exp(−(x−μ)²/(2σ²))

Shape: symmetric, bell-shaped, unimodal. Mean = Median = Mode. Symmetric about the mean.

68–95–99.7 Rule: ±1σ from mean → ~68% of data ±2σ from mean → ~95% of data ±3σ from mean → ~99.7% of data
Standard Normal Z: If X ~ N(μ, σ²), then Z = (X − μ)/σ ~ N(0, 1). Use a single Z-table for all normal distributions!

The normal distribution is preferred because its properties are well-understood and many other distributions tend to it under certain conditions (e.g., t-distribution as df → ∞).

04Population vs Sample — Estimation

ConceptPopulationSample
Meanμ (true, unknown)x̄ = Σxᵢ/n (estimator)
Varianceσ² (true, unknown)s² = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²/(n−1) (estimator)
TypeParameterStatistic / Point Estimate
Why n−1? Degrees of freedom. Among n deviations (xᵢ−x̄), they must sum to 0 — so only n−1 are independent. Dividing by n−1 gives an unbiased estimate of σ².

Good sampling rules: Independent observations, each element has equal probability of selection, all from the same distribution.

05Sampling Distribution of the Mean

x̄ is itself a random variable. Take many samples of size n — you get a distribution of sample means.

E[x̄] = μ (mean of sample means = population mean) Var[x̄] = σ²/n (variance of sample means = σ²/n) SD[x̄] = σ/√n (standard error of the mean)
Intuition: Larger sample → smaller variance of means → more precise estimates. A sample of size 100 gives 10× more precise estimates than size 1 (in terms of SD).
If population is normal: x̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n) for ANY sample size n.

06Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

CLT Statement: Even if the parent population is NOT normal, the sampling distribution of the mean approaches normality as n increases (n > 30 is the rule of thumb).

This is extremely useful — no matter how weird your data distribution is, with large enough samples, statistical inference based on normality works.

FALSE statements about CLT (exam favorite): "If parent is normal, sampling distribution is NOT normal for n<30" — FALSE. If parent is normal, sampling dist IS always normal regardless of n. "If parent is not normal, sampling dist is also not normal regardless of n" — FALSE (CLT fixes this for large n).

07Confidence Intervals

A range estimate for the population parameter μ. 100(1−α)% CI when σ is known:

x̄ − z(α/2)·σ/√n ≤ μ ≤ x̄ + z(α/2)·σ/√n α = level of significance (usually 0.05 for 95% CI) z(α/2) = upper 100α/2% point of N(0,1)
Meaning: If you take 100 random samples and compute 95% CIs from each, ~95 of those intervals will contain the true μ. Note: μ is fixed; the interval varies.

Broad vs narrow CI: Wide CI = more confident but less precise (train arrives between 1pm–5pm). Narrow CI = precise but less confident (2pm–2:15pm). There's a trade-off.

t-distribution: When σ² is unknown AND sample size is small, use t = (x̄−μ)/(s/√n) with n−1 degrees of freedom.

08Chi-Square & F Distributions

Chi-Square (χ²)

χ² = (n−1)s²/σ² with n−1 degrees of freedom. Used for confidence intervals on variance. Only positive values (right-skewed).

F Distribution

F = (S₁²/σ₁²)/(S₂²/σ₂²) — ratio of two chi-squares scaled by their DoF. Has two sets of DoF: numerator (m₁) and denominator (m₂).

F in DOE: Compare variation due to factor changes vs variation due to random error. High F = factor is significant. P-value = probability of committing a Type I error.
Hypothesis Testing

09Hypothesis Testing — H₀ vs H₁

Hypothesis testing makes decisions about population parameters using sample data.

Null Hypothesis H₀

"Status quo." E.g., "new process is NOT better than existing." The claim we test against.

Alternate Hypothesis H₁

Contradicts H₀. If H₀ is rejected → H₁ is automatically accepted.

Decision \ RealityH₀ is TRUEH₀ is FALSE
Do not reject H₀✓ CorrectType II Error (β)
Reject H₀Type I Error (α) ← MORE SERIOUS✓ Correct
Type I > Type II in severity: Innocent person convicted (wrongly rejecting innocence = Type I). Guilty person freed (wrongly not convicting = Type II). Type I = discarding a perfectly good process for a new unproven one.

Scope of hypothesis testing: concerns parameters of probability distribution of the population, not the sample. It relies on data contained in the sample taken from the population.

ANOVA & Experimental Design

10ANOVA Table — Analysis of Variance

Everything in DOE reduces to comparing variances. One-factor ANOVA:

TreatmentsSS_Treatmenta − 1MS_T = SS_T/(a−1)MS_T / MS_E
ErrorSS_Errora(n−1)MS_E = SS_E/[a(n−1)]
TotalSS_Totalan−1

Two-factor ANOVA adds: SS_A, SS_B, SS_AB (interaction). Interaction DoF = (a−1)(b−1).

Key: High F for a factor → that factor significantly affects the response. High F for AB → interaction is significant. If interaction plot lines are parallel → no interaction; if they cross/converge/diverge → interaction exists.

11Randomization in Experiments

Run experiments in a random order (not systematic). Why? Uncontrolled lurking effects (e.g., temperature build-up, instrument drift, operator fatigue) get evenly distributed across all treatments rather than systematically biasing one treatment.

Example: Testing 3 toners on a photocopier sequentially — if the copier heats up during runs, Toner C always gets hotter conditions. Randomization prevents this bias.

12Factorial Design (2ⁿ)

n factors, each at 2 levels (Low and High). Total runs = 2ⁿ.

  • Economical — maximum information from minimum runs compared to one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT).
  • Captures interactions — OFAT misses interaction effects between factors.
  • Both qualitative and quantitative factors can be analyzed together.
Interaction: Factor A's effect on response depends on the level of Factor B. If changing A from low→high gives different response at B_low vs B_high, they interact. Both main effects AND interaction effects matter.
Regression & Response Surface

13Linear Regression Model

y = β₀ + β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + ... + βₖXₖ + ε β₀ → intercept β₁..βₖ → partial regression coefficients (k+1 unknowns total) ε → random error ~ N(0, σ²)

Matrix form: y = Xβ + ε. Least-squares solution: β̂ = (X'X)⁻¹ X'y

Coefficient of Determination R²: Proportion of total variability explained by the model.
R² = 1 − SS_Error/SS_Total = SS_Regression/SS_Total. R² = 1 when SS_Error = 0 (perfect fit).
Don't chase R²=1! Adding useless parameters always inflates R². Use Adjusted R² = 1 − (MS_Error/MS_Total), which penalizes for extra parameters. If Adj R² decreases when you add a term, that term is useless.

14Second-Order Models & RSM

When the experimental space has peaks/valleys (non-linear response), use second-order models:

y = β₀ + Σβᵢxᵢ + ΣΣβᵢⱼxᵢxⱼ + Σβᵢᵢxᵢ² + ε interactions quadratic terms

Response Surface Methodology (RSM) finds the direction of steepest ascent/descent to reach the optimum efficiently.

Central Composite Design (CCD): Combines 2ⁿ factorial points + 2k axial points + center points. Center points detect curvature; axial points estimate individual quadratic terms (β₁₁, β₂₂, etc.).

Canonical Analysis (Eigenvalues of B matrix):
  • All eigenvalues negative → stationary point is a maximum
  • All eigenvalues positive → stationary point is a minimum
  • Mixed signs → saddle point

What is Research?

15Classroom vs Research Environment

ClassroomResearch
Problems are well-definedProblems are open-ended/ambiguous
Involves only one unknown variable (given 3, find 4th)Multiple variables; scholar must define and justify assumptions (given 1, define the other 3)
Fixed solutions existMultiple valid approaches; boundaries are fuzzy
Definition of Research: Conducting systematic scientific investigations to uncover the truth about a specific problem. NOT "repeating a search process" or "just discovering solutions."

16Key Research Concepts

Interdisciplinarity

Integrating knowledge from multiple fields (Physics + Mech + Bio + Civil etc.) to solve complex problems. Modern problems rarely fit into single "vertical silos."

Primary Objectives

(1) Acquiring new knowledge, (2) Staying updated with advances, (3) Systematic examination and critical analysis. → All of the above.

Purpose in Education

To contribute to becoming a respected educational professional. Not to diminish social status or suppress development.

Starting Point

Primary requirement when starting research: Establishing a research question (not data analysis or writing thesis first).

17Interdisciplinary Research

Primary objective of interdisciplinary research: To incorporate a comprehensive methodology by merging two or more fields — NOT to complicate problems or diminish focus.

Applied Mechanics at IIT Madras: 3 core groups — Fluid Mechanics, Solid Mechanics, Biomechanics/Biomedical Engineering. Faculty from 8 different undergraduate backgrounds. Cutting-edge tools: DNS of combustion, nano-indentation, atomic force microscopy (AFM).

India-Scale Thinking: Not all critical problems need the newest tools — innovative application of existing methods to strategic problems (DRDO, ISRO, rural development).

18Research Scholar's Journey

The big challenge: Transitioning from structured course work to open-ended research. Students accustomed to "given problem, find answer" must shift to "define the problem, justify your assumptions."

  • Approach it as a positive challenge, not an obstacle.
  • Embrace interdisciplinary work — the world doesn't fit into vertical silos.
  • Advisor meetings: Weekly initially; frequency adjusts as independence grows. Define the interaction pattern with your advisor — it's a two-person agreement.
  • Peer group discussion is as important as meeting the guide.
Success metrics for a PhD graduate: (1) Clear understanding and ability to explain work to others, (2) Identification of limitations and boundaries, (3) Setting goals for future research, (4) Ability to carry out research independently. — NOT dependence on others.

19Experimental Research Processes

NOT required in experimental research: Manipulating results. Required processes: controlling variables, making observations, collecting references.
Why manipulation is discouraged: It distorts validity and reliability of findings, undermines integrity and credibility of the researcher, and hampers progress of knowledge. → All of the above.

Practice MCQs

NPTEL-style questions. Some have more than one correct answer — marked with MULTI

Q1 — Statistics Fundamentals
A normal probability distribution function is symmetric about —
  • A Standard Deviation (SD)
  • B Mean
  • C SD × Mean
  • D SD − Mean
B — Mean. The normal distribution is symmetric about its mean μ. The area below and above the mean is exactly 50% each. SD controls the spread (width), not the center of symmetry.
Q2 — Regression
Coefficient of Determination (R²) is said to be unity when —
  • A Error Sum of Squares is zero
  • B Total Sum of Squares is zero
  • C Regression Sum of Squares is zero
  • D Regression SS is same as Total SS
A and D are both correct (they mean the same thing). R² = SS_Regression/SS_Total = 1 when SS_Error = 0, which is equivalent to SS_Regression = SS_Total (since SS_Total = SS_Regression + SS_Error).
Q3 — PDF Properties MULTI
Identify the TRUE statements for a continuous probability distribution f(x):
  • A ∫₋∞^∞ f(x)dx = 1
  • B ∫₋∞^∞ (x−μ)² f(x)dx = σ² where μ is mean and σ² is variance
  • C ∫ₐᵃ f(x)dx = 0 where a is a constant > 0
  • D ∫₋∞^∞ (x−μ)f(x)dx = 0
All four — A, B, C, D — are TRUE. A = total probability = 1. B = definition of variance. C = integral over zero-width interval = 0. D = E[(X−μ)] = E[X]−μ = μ−μ = 0 (expected deviation from mean is zero).
Q4 — Statistics Truths MULTI
Identify the TRUE statements from the following:
  • A In a collection of positive numbers, the arithmetic mean can become negative
  • B Two collections with different numbers may have the same mean but different standard deviations
  • C Standard deviation of a collection of negative numbers is negative
  • D When a population has exactly one number (say 10), the standard deviation is undefined
Only B is TRUE. A: All positive numbers → mean is positive, not negative. B: Correct — e.g., {1,3} and {2,2} both have mean 2 but different SDs. C: SD = √(variance) and variance is always ≥ 0, so SD is always ≥ 0, never negative. D: Population with one number has SD = 0 (not undefined).
Q5 — Quartiles & Medians
Data: 2.3, 7.2, 3.7, 4.6, 5.0, 7.0, 3.7, 4.9, 4.2. The second quartile (Q2) equals —
  • A The mean
  • B The median
  • C Twice the mean
  • D 0.5
B — The median. Q2 = second quartile = 50th percentile = median, by definition. The median of this dataset (sorted: 2.3, 3.7, 3.7, 4.2, 4.6, 4.9, 5.0, 7.0, 7.2) is the 5th value = 4.6.
Q6 — Arithmetic Progression Mean
100 numbers forming an AP with a = 5 and d = 3. The mean is:
  • A 100
  • B 148.5
  • C 153.5
  • D 305
C — 153.5. Mean of AP = a + (n−1)d/2 = 5 + (99)(3)/2 = 5 + 148.5 = 153.5. Alternatively: last term = 5 + 99×3 = 302. Mean = (first + last)/2 = (5+302)/2 = 307/2 = 153.5.
Q7 — Central Limit Theorem MULTI
Pick the FALSE statements about CLT (assuming truly random samples):
  • A If parent distribution is normal, sampling distribution is NOT normal if n < 30
  • B If parent distribution is normal, sampling distribution is NOT normal if n > 30
  • C If parent distribution is NOT normal, sampling distribution is normal if n < 30
  • D If parent distribution is NOT normal, sampling distribution is also NOT normal regardless of n
ALL FOUR (A, B, C, D) are FALSE. Correct facts: If parent is normal → sampling dist is ALWAYS normal (any n). If parent is not normal → sampling dist approaches normal for LARGE n (n > 30). So A and B are false (normal parent → sampling always normal). C is false (non-normal parent with n<30 doesn't guarantee normality). D is false (CLT ensures it does become normal for large n).
Q8 — Standard Normal
If P(−0.76 ≤ Z ≤ 0.76) = 0.553 for standard normal, what is P(Z < −0.76)?
  • A 0.553
  • B 0.447
  • C 0.224
  • D 0.276
C — 0.224. By symmetry, P(Z < −0.76) = P(Z > 0.76) = (1 − 0.553)/2 = 0.447/2 = 0.2235 ≈ 0.224.
Q9 — Sample Size
Population σ = 0.50 mm. Minimum sample size needed for sample σ to be 0.125 mm:
  • A 4
  • B 16
  • C 25
  • D 8
B — 16. Since Var[x̄] = σ²/n, the standard deviation of the sample mean = σ/√n. We need σ/√n = 0.125, so √n = 0.50/0.125 = 4, therefore n = 16.
Q10 — RSM Eigenvalues
In RSM Canonical Analysis with two factors, maximum response is achieved when eigenvalues of the B matrix are —
  • A All positive
  • B All negative
  • C All zero
  • D One positive and one negative
B — All negative. In RSM, if all eigenvalues of B are negative, the stationary point is a maximum. All positive → minimum (like a valley/bowl). Mixed → saddle point. All zero → indeterminate.

  • B Classroom: open-ended; Research: well-defined solutions
  • C Classroom: multiple variables; Research: one variable
  • D Classroom: one variable; Research: fewer given variables
A — Classroom: problems are well-defined and involve only one variable; Research: problems have multiple variables and require the scholar to find their relationships.

Q13 — Experimental Research
Which process is typically NOT required in experimental research?
  • A Controlling variables
  • B Making observations
  • C Collecting references
  • D Manipulating results
D — Manipulating results. This is the one thing explicitly NOT done in research. Manipulation distorts validity, undermines integrity, and hampers knowledge advancement.
Q14 — Hypothesis Testing
Scope of hypothesis testing concerns with — MULTI
  • A Parameters of probability distribution of population
  • B Parameters of probability distribution of sample
  • C It relies on data contained in the sample taken from population
  • D All of the above
A and C are correct (NOT B). Hypothesis testing concerns parameters of the POPULATION distribution (not sample), and it uses data from the sample. Note: Option D says "all of the above" which would include B (wrong), so the best answers are A and C. Some versions of this question accept only A as the primary answer.
Q15 — Probability Distribution
The probability density function (PDF) describes the following:
  • A Probability of a phenomenon occurring at macro scale
  • B Probability of a phenomenon occurring at micro scale
  • C Distribution of probabilities in the continuous random variable domain
  • D Distribution of probabilities in some undefined variables
C — Distribution of probabilities in the continuous random variable domain. A PDF maps each value of a continuous random variable to a relative likelihood (not exact probability), and the area under it gives actual probabilities.

What is the primary objective of interdisciplinary research?
  • A To complicate research problems
  • B To incorporate a comprehensive methodology by merging two or more fields
  • C To provide an introduction to traditional research methodology
  • D To diminish the focus on a single subject within the research domain
B — To incorporate a comprehensive methodology by merging two or more fields.
Q17 — ANOVA & F-test
In a two-factor ANOVA, if the interaction plot shows two parallel lines for factors A and B, this means:
  • A A strong interaction exists between A and B
  • B No interaction exists between A and B
  • C Factor A is insignificant
  • D Factor B is insignificant
B — No interaction exists between A and B. Parallel lines mean the effect of changing factor A is the same regardless of the level of factor B, and vice versa. Intersecting or converging/diverging lines indicate interaction.

Q20 — Discrete Probability Distribution (Calculation)
Discrete distribution: p(1)=0.321, p(2)=0.421, p(3)=0.209, p(4)=0.049. (Sum must =1, so p(5)=0.) Find the mean and variance.
  • A Mean = 1.98, Variance = 0.72
  • B Mean = 2.50, Variance = 1.20
  • C Mean = 1.50, Variance = 0.55
  • D Mean = 2.10, Variance = 0.85
A — Mean = 1.98, Variance = 0.72.
Mean = 1×0.321 + 2×0.421 + 3×0.209 + 4×0.049 = 0.321 + 0.842 + 0.627 + 0.196 = 1.986 ≈ 1.98
E[X²] = 1×0.321 + 4×0.421 + 9×0.209 + 16×0.049 = 0.321 + 1.684 + 1.881 + 0.784 = 4.67
Var = E[X²] − (E[X])² = 4.67 − (1.986)² = 4.67 − 3.944 = 0.726 ≈ 0.72
