Week 1

Introduction to the World of Research & Experimental Skills

🔬 What is Research?

Research is fundamentally different from coursework. It is a transition from being a consumer of knowledge to a producer of knowledge.

Einstein Quote: "If we knew what we were doing, it would not be called research, would it?"
📘 Course-Based Degree

Structured program. Learning established knowledge through courses, assignments, exams. Everyone aims for similar, correct answers. Path is well-defined.

🔬 Research-Based Degree

Novel contribution to field. Self-driven discovery, experimentation, validation. Creating unique, original work. A qualitative leap — training to become an independent researcher.

Textbook vs Research Paper

  • Textbooks provide accumulated knowledge; well-established content
  • Research papers explore new frontiers; cutting-edge, possibly uncertain
  • If a textbook exists for your exact research topic, you're probably too late!

Incremental vs Path-breaking Research

Incremental ResearchPath-breaking Research
Small, structured additions to existing knowledge Fundamentally changes a field's direction
Most research is this type Often happens by serendipity through steady incremental work
Systematic, methodical Requires a "prepared mind"
Key Exam Point: Incremental research leads to path-breaking research. They are NOT unrelated — path-breaking work builds on incremental foundations.

🗺️ Selecting a Research Area

The analogy: research is like exploring an unknown city. You know you want to visit, but you don't know which monuments will fascinate you until you start exploring.

How to Choose

  1. Start with genuine interest — Passion is your engine. Don't let job prospects or trends be the primary driver. Intrinsic motivation is non-negotiable.
  2. Trace your interest — From an undergraduate course that resonated, general reading that sparked curiosity, or desire for societal impact.
  3. Specialize beyond the textbook — Don't pick a broad subject like "signals and systems." True research is highly specialized. Dig into niche sub-fields.
  4. Conduct a literature survey — This is the important early step involving trial and error to identify interesting topics.
Exam Alert: Important factors for selecting a research area = "The appeal and interest in the area of study and the impact it has on the scientific community." NOT convenience of location, schedule flexibility, or number of students.

👨‍🏫 Selecting an Advisor

A PhD can last 4–7 years, making this one of the longest and most intense professional partnerships you'll have.

What to Do

  • Investigate: Read potential advisor's publications. Does their work excite you?
  • Communicate: Have honest conversations. Express your interests, ask about mentorship style.
  • Evaluate compatibility: Working style (hands-on, theoretical, experimental?), personality & energy

Role of an Advisor

Correct Answer: "To provide guidance when the student encounters difficulties or loses direction." — NOT day-to-day instructions, NOT doing all research, NOT ensuring strict adherence to initial plan.

A good guide helps you understand the "lay of the land" — is a research area too crowded? Is a problem worth solving? They provide historical context and strategic insight.

📓 Day-to-Day Research Practice

Small Steps Matter

  • Most daily work = small steps. Setting up experiments may take 2–3 weeks — that's normal, not wasted time.
  • Reproducing existing work is a valid and valuable starting point — it makes you familiar with the area.
  • Data should be believable, defensible, and reproducible.

The Research Notebook

  • Note down every observation, every small result, every plot — electronically or on paper.
  • These observations build up over time to something significant.
  • Be excited and proud of small results — this keeps you thinking about extensions.

Discipline & Regular Hours

Misconception busted: Working late nights ≠ great work. Regular 8am–5pm with evenings off can produce excellent research. What matters is regular, disciplined hours.

Simulations = Experiments

  • Same rules of Design of Experiments apply to simulations
  • Simulators are "deadly weapons" — can produce massive amounts of data, only a fraction useful
  • Must understand solver settings, engine underneath, right tools for the purpose
Exam Point: Attitude for research = "A steady and incremental approach while keeping the long-term goal in mind." NOT urgency/impatience, NOT OCD, NOT deviating from objectives.

👥 Peer Review & Validation

Research is NOT Democratic

  • One expert can prove everyone before wrong — their idea then prevails.
  • Not about majority agreement, not about authority of famous names.
  • Peers review each other and hold something as true until disproved.

Validating Your Results

  1. Self-validation: Conviction must begin from YOU. Validate qualitatively and quantitatively.
  2. Advisor discussion: Share and discuss — maybe you're right, maybe you need correction.
  3. Group presentation: Share with group mates for critical feedback — even if advisor doesn't insist on it.
  4. Peer community: Communicate through conferences and papers — identify reputable journals and conferences run by professional societies (e.g., IEEE).

Honesty & Reproducibility

  • You must be the most critical examiner of your own work
  • There is no "final result" — research fields don't end
  • Check if assumptions are not too restrictive
  • Ensure qualitative sense-making
  • Results should be reproducible — publish code, post data if not confidential
Key Quote (Thomas Huxley): "Science is simply common sense at its best, that is, rigidly accurate in observation, and merciless to fallacy in logic."

🧠 Learning Paradigms in Research

ParadigmDescriptionExampleTime Scale
Just-in-Time Learn only what's needed for immediate task Learning a MATLAB script to make one plot Short
Strategic Learn up to the extent needed to solve a specific problem Understanding enough to solve a problem, not the whole theory Medium
In-Depth Go to the bottom of knowledge; get into long-term memory Your core research area — deep, thorough mastery Long
PhD Journey: Starts with Just-in-Time learning → converges to In-Depth learning. Your attitude of learning MUST change from exam-prep mode when you enter research.

🤝 Teamwork in Research

  • Research is rarely done in complete isolation — you work in groups
  • Some you learn from, some you teach — roles reverse over time
  • Hiding resources and not sharing = bad long-term strategy
  • Know how to share credit in publications
  • Joining an active group (publishing regularly) is "positively contagious"
Newton Quote: "If I have seen further than other men, it is because I have stood on the shoulder of giants." — Teamwork at the grandest scale.

Motivation: Daniel Pink's Framework (Book: "Drive")

🎯 Mastery

Sense of getting better over time — skills, techniques, methodologies improve

🌍 Purpose

Larger purpose for work — curiosity, improvement, betterment of society

🔓 Autonomy

Freedom to choose techniques, methodologies, specific problems to solve

💡 Result

Researchers are intrinsically motivated when all three align

Important characteristics for researchers: "Spark of interest and spirit of learning" — NOT super intelligence, NOT no work ethic, NOT just communication skills.
Most appropriate motivation: "To gain knowledge that advances understanding and enables meaningful impact."
Einstein closing quote: "Imagination is more important than knowledge."

⚗️ Experimental Skills

Research is broadly theoretical or experimental. Experiments are supreme — they validate theories. But they must be run correctly.

Core Principle: Experiments are supreme in the hierarchy of science. If theory doesn't match well-run experiments, the theory is incomplete. But this assumes you've run the experiment CORRECTLY.

🌡️ Temperature Measurement

  • Common error: Trusting the furnace display blindly. The thermocouple may be located far from your sample.
  • The furnace may show 800°C, but sample could be at 750°C due to gas flow, heating element positions, etc.
  • Solution: Add a second thermocouple positioned directly above your sample. Adjust furnace set-point accordingly.
  • Temperature gradients exist both vertically and horizontally in furnaces.

💧 Humidity Measurement

  • Common error: Assuming humidifier output = 100% RH. Design and flow rate affect efficiency.
  • Solution: Use a desiccant box — weigh before and after to measure actual water content.
  • Path matters: If the tube between humidifier and experiment drops to room temperature, water condenses out as liquid — no longer in vapor phase!
  • Must heat-trace the path to maintain temperature above dew point.

⚡ Current & Voltage Measurement

  • Current: Use a clamp-on meter to independently verify current without disturbing the experiment.
  • Voltage — Contact Resistance Problem:
❌ Two-Probe Measurement

Same two contact points for current AND voltage. Measures Rcontact1 + Rsample + Rcontact2. Can be off by 20-30%!

✅ Four-Probe Measurement

Current through outer two probes, voltage measured at inner two probes. Eliminates contact resistance error (3-4 orders of magnitude smaller).

🔑 Golden Rule of Experimental Skills

For EVERY measurement in EVERY experiment:
  1. Understand HOW the instrument makes its measurement
  2. Identify WHERE the sensor is located
  3. Find possible sources of error
  4. Compensate and correct for those errors
  5. Cross-check with independent methods
  6. Feel confident to DEFEND your measurements

Week 2

Literature Survey — Search, Organize, and Reference

📚 What is a Literature Survey?

DISCLAIMER: Literature survey is NOT just Googling for information. Google looks at private websites. Literature survey = survey of technical, authoritative literature from universities, research labs, and scientific enterprises. Google Scholar is the closest Google equivalent.

Why Do Literature Survey?

  • Know what approaches others have used and where gaps exist
  • Avoid duplication — even unintentional duplication is unacceptable in research degrees
  • Clarify controversial results
  • Establish state-of-the-art and build on others' work
  • Place your study in a current context where peers see it as valuable
  • Establish context and background for your own work
  • Point out anomalies and gaps

Novelty Principle

Novel technique + familiar area

A new method applied to a well-known problem = good contribution

Familiar technique + novel area

A known method applied to a brand new problem = also valuable

Exam Point: Literature review must be carried out throughout the research process — NOT only at the start, NOT only at the end, NOT only while publishing.

Reliability Hierarchy

Exam Point: Compared to peer-reviewed journals, websites are less likely to be reliable. NOT equally reliable, NOT completely incorrect, NOT more reliable.

📖 Key Terminology

TermFull FormWhat It DoesKey Exam Facts
ISBN International Standard Book Number Unique identifier for books Without ISBN, book is not traceable
ISSN International Standard Serial Number Unique identifier for journals, newspapers, conference proceedings Comes in ranges; also for newspapers
DOI Digital Object Identifier Persistent, unique alphanumeric string for online documents Stable even if URL changes. Sufficient to retrieve ALL other bibliographic details. Best way to refer to internet location.
Impact Factor Journal Impact Factor Average citations to recent articles in a journal Controversial. Cannot compare across different research areas. Only relative within same field.
h-index — Author has h-index of n if n papers have ≥ n citations each Also controversial. Context-dependent. Higher h-index = more widely read/referred.
Citation Index — How many times a specific article has been cited "Citation classics" = articles with hundreds of citations
Critical Exam Points about DOI:
  • DOI is sufficient to retrieve all other bibliographic details of an article ✅
  • DOI link is the best way to refer to internet location of a published article ✅
  • DOI is necessary to retrieve all details of a digitally published article ✅
  • Author+Journal or Author+Year alone is NOT sufficient ❌

What Publishers Allow Without Subscription

Answer: Title, Authors, Volume, Issue, Page number and DOI — these are generally allowed free. Abstract and Full text typically require subscription.

Impact of an Article

Measure of impact: Number of articles referring to it (citations). NOT likes, views, or downloads.

🔍 Literature Search Strategies

StrategyMethodDirectionGoal
1. Keyword Search Use specific words, author names, or article-defined keywords — Cast a wide net to find initial papers. Use OR Boolean to combine synonyms.
2. Backward Chronological Start with recent article → examine its reference list → go back in time recursively ⬅️ Past Understand historical development and foundational theories
3. Forward Chronological Start with key foundational paper → find all papers that cited it → go forward in time ➡️ Future Trace evolution of idea, identify current state-of-the-art

Search Tools & Databases

  • Google Scholar — closest to Google for academic search
  • INSPEC, COMPENDEX — subscription databases for engineering
  • PubMed — medical community
  • MathSciNet — mathematics
  • Web of Science (Thomson ISI) — citation database, forward search
  • Scopus (Elsevier) — citation database, forward search

Sorting Search Results

  • Newest first: See latest publications
  • Oldest first: See how field started
  • Highest citations: Find "citation classics" — most important works
  • Lowest citations: Find potentially overlooked but valuable papers
  • Usage count: See what peers on the portal are reading

Choosing References for Publication

Criterion for including a reference: The article is a prior work directly relating to the topic. NOT because authored by likely reviewers, well-known people, or appeared in well-known journal (those alone aren't sufficient criteria).

🛠️ Literature Survey Tools

Major Publishers (80% of journals)

  • Elsevier → ScienceDirect portal
  • Springer → SpringerLink portal
  • Taylor & Francis, Nature Publications, Maney Publishing
  • Society journals: APS, AIP, IEEE, etc.

Web of Science Workflow

  1. Check subscription with librarian (IP-based or roaming login)
  2. Register & login to Web of Science + EndNote Web (two tabs)
  3. Search using keywords with OR Boolean operator
  4. Sort results (by date, citations, relevance, usage count)
  5. Check boxes to select → "Add to Marked List"
  6. Export Marked List to EndNote (select Author/Title/Source + Abstract)
  7. In EndNote: collect unfiled items → create group → export as BibTeX (.bib)

Scopus Workflow

  1. Check subscription with librarian
  2. Register & login to Scopus
  3. Search with keywords, choose fields carefully
  4. Sort results, select important ones → "Save to List"
  5. Export directly as BibTeX format (citations + abstracts)
  6. File downloads as Scopus.bib → rename meaningfully

RSS/Content Subscription

Enroll email with publishers → new articles pushed to inbox. Basic info (title, authors, volume, issue, page number) available even without full subscription.

Special Interest Group Websites

Example: imechanica.org for mechanical engineering — discusses ongoing work in the domain.

📎 Reference Management

Why Use a Reference Manager?

  • Articles may have 10-30 references; theses may have 100+
  • Avoid typographical errors from manual entry
  • Ensure every cited work is listed in bibliography
  • Auto-handle sequencing when text is rearranged
  • Switch between citation styles (APA, MLA, numbered, etc.) effortlessly

Bibliographic Data Formats

BibTeX (.bib)

Pure text, human-readable. Each entry starts with @article. Native for LaTeX and JabRef. Can cut/paste/merge in Notepad.

RIS Format

Another popular format. Used by many reference managers.

JabRef (Recommended — Free & Open Source)

  • Java-based, runs on any platform
  • Displays .bib file as a spreadsheet (rows = entries, columns = fields)
  • Can search, sort, add notes, split/merge files
  • Can export BibTeX → XML (for MS Word)
  • Files reside on your desktop — work offline at your own pace
  • Other managers: Zotero, Mendeley (some paid, some free)

Using References in Documents

LaTeX + BibTeX

Keep .bib file in same folder as .tex file. Use \bibliography{filename} and \bibliographystyle{unsrt}. Cite with \cite{key}. Compile: LaTeX → BibTeX → LaTeX × 2.

MS Word

Convert .bib → XML via JabRef. References → Manage Sources → Browse XML. Copy references to current list. Insert Citation at any point. Insert Bibliography at end.

Handling Retracted References

If a cited article is retracted before your paper goes to press: Remove the retracted reference and modify the text and citations accordingly. Do NOT just add a note, ignore it, or replace with same author's other article.

Identifying Fake Journals

Strongest indicator of a fake journal: The journal does not have an editorial board or review process. Page charges alone, similar name to reputed journal, or free articles are not definitive indicators.

📋 Week 2 — Master Summary

  • Not Googling: Literature survey = formal process using authoritative sources
  • Goals: Avoid duplication, identify gaps, prove novelty, establish context
  • Continuous process: Throughout research, not just at start
  • Sources: Primary (journals, proceedings) → Secondary (reviews, textbooks) → Tertiary (bibliographies, dictionaries)
  • Access: Open access, INDEST, library subscriptions, peer-sharing (ResearchGate, Academia.edu)
  • DOI: The single most important identifier — sufficient to retrieve ALL details, best for web references
  • ISBN/ISSN: For books/serials respectively — essential for traceability
  • Impact Factor: Controversial, relative within a field only, not across fields
  • h-index: n papers cited ≥ n times. Context-dependent
  • Article impact: Measured by number of articles citing it
  • Search strategies: Keyword + Backward (references list → past) + Forward (citations → future)
  • Tools: Web of Science, Scopus for citation data; JabRef for management
  • Free fields from publishers: Title, Authors, Volume, Issue, Page number, DOI
  • Reference criterion: Prior work directly relating to the topic
  • Retracted article: Remove and modify text/citations
  • Fake journal sign: No editorial board or review process
  • Websites < peer-reviewed journals in reliability
